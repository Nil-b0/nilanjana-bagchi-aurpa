Classical and Quantum Fisher Information

This project presents a minimal computational framework comparing classical Fisher information, used extensively in cosmological parameter inference, with quantum Fisher information arising from parameterized quantum states.

Using the Hubble constant H0 as a representative cosmological parameter, the work highlights how classical and quantum Cramér–Rao bounds emerge and how quantum information degrades under noise.

Conceptual Motivation

In cosmology, Fisher information quantifies the sensitivity of observational likelihoods to underlying model parameters and determines local precision bounds. Independently, quantum information theory introduces the Quantum Fisher Information (QFI), which sets the ultimate precision limit for parameters encoded in quantum states.

Despite their structural similarity, these two quantities are rarely studied together in a transparent computational setting. This project provides a controlled bridge between the two frameworks.

Method Overview

A toy Gaussian likelihood is constructed for the Hubble constant, yielding a constant classical Fisher information and a fixed classical Cramér–Rao bound.

The same parameter is encoded into a single-qubit quantum circuit using a parameterized rotation. The Quantum Fisher Information is evaluated analytically and numerically, and extended to mixed states in the presence of depolarizing noise.

Results

Discussion

In the noiseless limit, the quantum encoding achieves the expected constant Quantum Fisher Information. As noise strength increases, the available quantum information degrades monotonically, leading to weaker quantum precision bounds, while the classical bound remains unchanged.

This work does not aim to demonstrate quantum computational advantage or improved cosmological constraints. Instead, it clarifies the structural parallels between classical and quantum information bounds in a minimal, reproducible setting.
