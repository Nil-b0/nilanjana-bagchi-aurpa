Cosmology

My work in cosmology focuses on reconstructing the expansion history of the Universe using observational data, with an emphasis on methods that reduce model dependence and explicitly quantify uncertainty.

I am particularly interested in extending cosmological analyses to higher redshifts using non-standard probes and in developing statistically robust techniques for inference from noisy and heterogeneous datasets.

Research Themes

  • Reconstruction of the cosmic expansion history from observational data, with emphasis on late-time acceleration and high-redshift behavior.
  • Use of non-standard cosmological probes, particularly Gamma Ray Bursts and Fast Radio Bursts, to extend distance measurements beyond the redshift range of Type Ia supernovae.
  • Model-independent and minimally parametric approaches to cosmological inference, aimed at reducing assumptions about the underlying cosmological model.
  • Quantification of statistical and systematic uncertainties in cosmological parameter estimation.
  • Information-theoretic limits on parameter inference, including both classical Fisher information and quantum Fisher information frameworks.

Methods

  • Gaussian Process regression for non-parametric reconstruction of cosmological observables such as the Hubble parameter, distance modulus, and dispersion measures.
  • Bayesian inference techniques, including Monte Carlo sampling, for parameter estimation and uncertainty propagation.
  • Neural network–based calibration of empirical relations, with particular focus on Gamma Ray Burst luminosity correlations.
  • Statistical resampling and error propagation techniques to assess the robustness of reconstructed cosmological functions.
  • Fisher matrix and information-based analyses to study theoretical limits on parameter precision and the impact of noise and systematics.
